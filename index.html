Tournament Selection

Probability that individual of rank i wins a k-tournament in a population of size N

Pt(i) = C(i−1, k−1) / C(N, k)
5
2

Rank 1 = worst, rank N = best. Higher k → sharper selection pressure.

⚖️

k = 1 — Uniform

All individuals equally likely. No selection pressure.

📈

k = 2 — Linear

Selection probability grows linearly with rank.

🎯

k = N — Deterministic

Only the best individual can win. Can destroy diversity.

Why does this matter?

Crossover alone cannot guarantee ergodicity. As M grows, the expected hyperrectangle covers a negligible fraction of the domain. Mutation (pm > 0) is the necessary ingredient.

Four-Component Offspring Kernel

The offspring density p(z′ | a, b) decomposes into four terms from expanding the crossover × mutation mixture

p(z′|a,b) = (i) pcpm ∫ U·φ̃ dz
           + (ii) pc(1−pm) · U(z′) · 1[z′∈R]
           + (iii) (1−pc)pm · φ̃(z′|a)
           + (iv) (1−pc)(1−pm) · δ(z′−a)
0.40 0.80
Irreducibility engine: term (iii) has full support on 𝒟 whenever pm > 0 and Σ is positive definite — the truncated Gaussian is strictly positive everywhere.
